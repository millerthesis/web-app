Examples

Here you will find examples of dynamic areas of change in the United States.

Washington, D.C.

The nation's capital is home to some of the most heavily-gentrifying zip codes in the country, according to an analysis by RentCafe. In fact, two of the top 20 gentrified zipcodes are located in D.C., with home values going up an average of 176 percent between 2000-2016. For decades, black residents were the majority in the city -- making up 70 percent of residents in 1980 -- until 2015, when that number fell to 48 percent.

St. Louis, MO

St. Louis is notable because it is the only Midwest city on RentCafe's list of most gentrified zipcodes between 2000-2016. Unlike other rapidly-gentrifying cities, the population of St. Louis city has declined over the last several decades (it peaked at 856,000 in 1950; it now has approximately 311,000 residents), and real estate prices remain relatively modest, with the average one-bedroom apartment renting for $800.

Fort Worth, TX

Fort Worth, the fifth-largest city in Texas, has seen home prices in some neighborhoods increase by as much as 320 percent between 2000-2016. While the median home cost just under $47,000 in 2000, it shot up to $198,400 in 2016, according to RentCafe. And the percent of people with bachelor's degrees increased along with household income in this same time, by 122 percent and 103 percent, respectively.

San Francisco, CA

San Francisco: where the average studio costs about $2,400 monthly and the median home is worth $1.61 million. Historically low and middle-income neighborhoods like the Tenderloin, Chinatown and Bayview are rapidly becoming more expensive, adding to the already dire housing crisis the city faces. Interesting, however, is that not all middle-income neighborhoods face the same level of gentrification; areas like the Sunset and Richmond districts have not shown a distinct increase in the number of wealthy residents.
